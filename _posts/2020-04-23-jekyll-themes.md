In the process of creating this website, I realized that Jekyll theming isn't as simple as it seems to be.

---

### Ecosystem
Jekyll is designed to be very extensible and light from the get-go. Markdown in, static HTML out. Users can add their own preferences and ideas too, using Jekyll plugins and custom themes. Interestingly, despite the many choices out there for theming a Jekyll site, the most accessible fall into two main categories:

* Lightweight, yet ugly
* Heavy

Now what do I mean by accessible? When a new user is online researching how to setup their new website, they are presented with a landslide of options ranging from visually bare to visually complex. These tend to come from a small handful of websites, all with similar offerings. These themes usually take 5 seconds to generate, which is extremely significant when making many changes to something like CSS or content.

### Alternatives
Let's assume a user has looked at their options and chosen to make a custom theme. The documentation is severely lacking. When setting up this website, I spent most of my time trying to figure out how to theme the thing from scratch. It's user-hostile and simply prevents new users from onboarding. I hope that at least my website can provide a solution to users looking for good examples.

### Solution
To solve this dilemma, two things would be extremely helpful:
* Improve documentation of theming processes and how they're implemented.
* Improve the theme structure itself or at least reduce the barrier to understanding it.

Luckily, most of the problems here can be solved with community involvement, so I hope to contribute my little part to the Jekyll docs and I hope you can too.
